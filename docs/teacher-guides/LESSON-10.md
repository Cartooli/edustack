# Teacher Guide: Lesson 10 — AI Helps You Code

> **Prep time: 15 minutes.** Prompt engineering lesson. The mid-class pause is key.

---

## What This Lesson Teaches

Students learn prompt engineering — the skill of writing clear, specific questions
that get useful answers from AI. They practice writing prompts, comparing results,
and iterating on their questions.

## What Students Will Build

A web page built with AI assistance where they:
- Write specific prompts to get CSS or JavaScript help
- Compare good vs. bad prompts and their results
- Iterate: ask, test, refine, ask again
- Document what they asked and what they got

**Expected result:** A page with at least 2 features they got help building through
well-crafted AI prompts.

## Before Class

- [ ] Computers are on and logged in
- [ ] If AI is disabled, prepare 5 pairs of good/bad prompt examples with responses
- [ ] You've read SKILL-10 completely
- [ ] Plan the mid-class pause (see timing below — this is critical)
- [ ] Optional: Print prompt comparison worksheet

## Timing (45-minute class)

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:05 | Review: "What makes a good question? What makes a bad one?" |
| 0:05–0:10 | Show 2 prompts, same goal, different quality. Compare results. |
| 0:10–0:25 | Students work through SKILL-10 prompt exercises |
| 0:25–0:30 | **MID-CLASS PAUSE:** Stop everyone. Compare good vs. bad prompts as a class. |
| 0:30–0:42 | Students continue building with AI-assisted prompts |
| 0:42–0:48 | Share: "What was your best prompt? What did you learn about asking?" |
| 0:48–0:50 | Key takeaway: "The quality of the answer depends on the quality of the question." |

## The Mid-Class Pause (Critical Moment)

At the 15-minute mark, stop the whole class. Ask 2–3 students to share their prompts.
Put a "bad" prompt and a "good" prompt side by side:

**Bad:** "make my page look cool"
**Good:** "How do I add a blue gradient background that goes from dark blue at the top
to light blue at the bottom using CSS?"

The second prompt gives the AI enough detail to give a useful, specific answer.
This comparison is the key learning moment.

## Common Questions

**"Why doesn't the AI understand what I want?"**
"The AI can't see your screen or read your mind. You need to describe what you have,
what you want, and what's not working. The more specific, the better."

**"Can I just paste my whole file and say 'fix it'?"**
"You could, but you won't learn much. Instead, describe the specific problem:
'My button doesn't change color on hover. Here's my CSS for the button...'"

**"The AI gave me code I don't understand."**
"Then you need to ask a follow-up: 'Can you explain what the flexbox line does?'
Never use code you don't understand — that's how bugs happen."

**"Is using AI cheating?"**
"Using AI to learn is like using a dictionary — it's a tool. The key is: do you
understand the code? Can you explain it? Can you modify it? If yes, you learned.
If no, you just copied."

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | What You'll See | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Prompts too vague | Generic, unhelpful responses | "Add details: what element? what property? what result?" |
| Copying without reading | Code that doesn't fit their project | "Read each line. Explain it to me before you use it." |
| Not testing AI code | Bugs from untested suggestions | "Paste it in, save, refresh. Does it work?" |
| Asking one big question | Overwhelming response | "Break it into smaller questions. One concept at a time." |

## Safety Notes for This Lesson

- Monitor prompts and responses — students are actively interacting with AI
- Common issue: students try to get AI to write their entire project. Redirect:
  "The AI is your helper, not your replacement."
- Watch for students sharing AI responses that contain errors — use these as
  teaching moments
- If AI is disabled, the teacher-as-AI approach from Lesson 09 still works here
- Remind students: never include personal information in prompts

---

*This lesson teaches the meta-skill of communicating with AI tools. Students who learn
to write good prompts will be better at every AI tool they use in the future — not just
EduBot.*
